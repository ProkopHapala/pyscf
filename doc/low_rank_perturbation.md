Yes — the unifying idea is **not really “coarse geometry”**, but **coarse subspaces that capture the slow/error-prone modes of the operator**. In elasticity this subspace has a beautiful geometric meaning: long-wavelength displacement fields of a mesh. In electrostatics and electronic structure the same idea exists, but the “coarse variables” are less obviously positions; they are moments, smooth charge densities, low-energy orbitals, local subspace coefficients, or approximate invariant subspaces.

The key multigrid principle is:

[
x = P x_c + x_{\rm fine}
]

where (P) prolongates coarse degrees of freedom into the fine space. Relaxation/Jacobi/Gauss-Seidel kills high-frequency error quickly, but leaves low-frequency error. The coarse solve attacks that low-frequency error because, on the coarse grid, it no longer looks low-frequency.

For elasticity, (x) is displacement.
For electrostatics, (x) may be potential, charge, induced dipoles, or QEq charges.
For electronic structure, (x) may be a wavefunction, density matrix, Green-function response vector, or correction vector.

## 1. Electrostatics: FMM is multigrid-like, but not exactly the same thing

Fast Multipole Method and multigrid share a hierarchy, but they accelerate different viewpoints.

For Poisson/electrostatics,

[
\nabla^2 \phi = -\rho/\epsilon_0
]

ordinary geometric multigrid is extremely natural: solve potential (\phi) on a coarse grid, interpolate to fine grid, smooth local errors. This is true multigrid.

FMM instead evaluates

[
\phi_i = \sum_j \frac{q_j}{|\mathbf r_i-\mathbf r_j|}
]

by grouping distant charges and representing them by multipoles. That is more like **hierarchical low-rank compression of the Coulomb matrix**:

[
K_{ij} = \frac{1}{|\mathbf r_i-\mathbf r_j|}
]

Distant blocks of this matrix are low-rank because far away you do not resolve individual charges; you see monopole, dipole, quadrupole, etc.

So yes: FMM is conceptually close to multigrid, but the coarse variables are not coarse grid charges in the same way as FEM elasticity. They are **multipole moments of charge clusters**.

For a cluster (C), instead of individual charges (q_i), you store

[
Q = \sum_{i\in C} q_i
]

[
\mathbf p = \sum_{i\in C} q_i(\mathbf r_i-\mathbf R_C)
]

[
Q_{\alpha\beta} = \sum_{i\in C} q_i
(r_i-R_C)*\alpha(r_i-R_C)*\beta
]

and so on. This is a coarse projection:

[
q_{\rm fine} \rightarrow {Q,\mathbf p,Q_{\alpha\beta},\ldots}_{\rm coarse}
]

The prolongation is the inverse operation approximately: a far-field potential generated by these multipoles is evaluated on fine points.

So for electrostatics, the “geometric interpretation” is:

> coarse variables are the low angular-frequency components of the charge distribution as seen from far away.

This is why electrostatics is extremely favorable for hierarchical methods. Coulomb is long-ranged but smooth at distance.

## 2. Charge equilibration / QEq: the analogy is even closer

QEq minimizes something like

[
E(q) =
\sum_i \chi_i q_i
+
\frac{1}{2}\sum_i \eta_i q_i^2
+
\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i\ne j} q_i J_{ij} q_j
]

with charge conservation

[
\sum_i q_i = Q_{\rm total}.
]

The linear system is approximately

[
(\eta + J)q = -\chi + \lambda \mathbf 1 .
]

Here (J_{ij}) is Coulomb-like. The hard modes and soft modes are different from elasticity, but there is still a natural coarse hierarchy.

A coarse cell does not need to know every atomic charge separately. At long range, it only needs the multipoles of the charge rearrangement. Therefore one can decompose charge into

[
q_i = q_i^{\rm local} + \sum_A P_{iA} Q_A
]

where (Q_A) is a coarse charge variable for block (A), and (P_{iA}) distributes that coarse charge over atoms in the block using weights.

For example, for a molecular fragment or spatial block:

[
Q_A = \sum_{i\in A} q_i
]

and maybe also dipole-like coarse DOFs:

[
p_{A\alpha} = \sum_{i\in A} q_i (r_i-R_A)_\alpha .
]

Then the coarse QEq problem asks: how much net charge and polarization should each fragment/block carry? The fine solve then redistributes charge locally according to electronegativities and hardnesses.

This is very similar to elasticity:

| Elasticity               | QEq / electrostatics                            |
| ------------------------ | ----------------------------------------------- |
| coarse node displacement | coarse block charge / dipole                    |
| fine vertex displacement | atomic charge                                   |
| long-wavelength bending  | long-wavelength charge/potential redistribution |
| local strain disorder    | intra-fragment charge rearrangement             |
| stiffness matrix         | Coulomb + hardness matrix                       |

The important difference is that elasticity has vector displacement DOFs living in real space, while QEq has scalar charge DOFs whose sum is constrained. But the multigrid logic survives.

A good practical two-level QEq decomposition would be:

[
q = P Q + q_\perp
]

where (P Q) is the coarse charge distribution and (q_\perp) is constrained to have zero monopole, and maybe zero dipole, inside each block. Then:

1. solve coarse electrostatics for (Q),
2. solve local block QEq corrections (q_\perp),
3. iterate.

That would be a true electrostatic analogue of coarse-grid elasticity.

## 3. Electronic structure: the same idea exists, but the coarse space is spectral, not purely geometric

For electronic structure, the problem is harder because the Hamiltonian is not just a smooth elliptic operator. It has oscillatory wavefunctions, nodal structure, band structure, local chemistry, near-degeneracies, and occupied/unoccupied separation.

Still, the multigrid idea is very much alive.

For a Schrödinger-type problem,

[
H\psi = E\psi
]

or your resolvent problem

[
(EI-H)r = b,
]

Jacobi fails near the spectrum because (EI-H) becomes nearly singular. If (E) is close to an eigenvalue (\varepsilon_n), then

[
(EI-H)^{-1}
===========

\sum_n
\frac{|\psi_n\rangle\langle \psi_n|}
{E-\varepsilon_n}.
]

The response is dominated by eigenvectors with (\varepsilon_n \approx E). That means the “slow modes” are not necessarily long-wavelength geometric modes; they are **near-resonant eigenstates**.

So the right coarse subspace is not simply a coarser real-space grid. It is a subspace (V) that approximates the relevant eigenvectors:

[
r \approx V y + r_\perp .
]

Then project:

[
V^\dagger(EI-H)V, y
===================

V^\dagger b .
]

This gives a small dense problem in the important subspace. The residual (r_\perp) can be treated by Jacobi, domain-local relaxation, or Krylov methods.

This is exactly the logic behind **deflation**, **coarse correction**, **subspace iteration**, **domain decomposition**, **multigrid eigensolvers**, and **Krylov-accelerated Green-function solvers**.

## 4. Why Jacobi fails for ((EI-H)r=b)

Suppose

[
H\psi_n = \varepsilon_n \psi_n.
]

Then in the eigenbasis,

[
(EI-H)r=b
]

becomes

[
(E-\varepsilon_n) r_n = b_n
]

so

[
r_n = \frac{b_n}{E-\varepsilon_n}.
]

If (E) is near some (\varepsilon_n), the denominator is small. The operator is ill-conditioned or indefinite. Jacobi works when the matrix is diagonally dominant or elliptic-like. But (EI-H) in the middle of the spectrum is not positive definite. It has both positive and negative eigenvalues:

[
E-\varepsilon_n >0
]

for states below (E), and

[
E-\varepsilon_n <0
]

for states above (E). Therefore the iteration matrix contains modes that are weakly damped or even amplified.

This is not merely a numerical inconvenience. Physically, the response really is resonant. You are trying to solve a driven system at an energy where it can sustain large standing-wave components.

Usually one adds broadening:

[
(E+i\eta-H)r=b.
]

Then

[
r_n =
\frac{b_n}{E+i\eta-\varepsilon_n}.
]

The imaginary part (\eta) regularizes the pole. Numerically this is much better, and physically it corresponds to lifetime broadening / absorbing boundary / finite spectral resolution.

## 5. The electronic analogue of coarse deformation modes

For elasticity, a good coarse basis is obvious: low-order spatial interpolation.

For electronic structure, useful coarse bases include several possibilities.

### A. Atomic orbitals / minimal basis as coarse grid

If your fine representation is a real-space grid or large basis, the coarse space can be atomic orbitals:

[
\psi(\mathbf r)
\approx
\sum_\mu c_\mu \phi_\mu(\mathbf r).
]

This is almost the electronic analogue of FEM shape functions. Atomic orbitals are interpolation functions centered on nuclei. The coarse coefficients (c_\mu) play the role of coarse nodal amplitudes.

For molecules, this is often better than geometric real-space coarsening because electronic states are not smooth everywhere; they have cusps, nodal planes, and chemical locality. Atom-centered functions already know the local singular/cusp structure.

So a natural electronic prolongation is:

[
P_{\mathbf r,\mu} = \phi_\mu(\mathbf r)
]

from AO coefficients to fine real-space values.

This is very close in spirit to multigrid, but the coarse basis is chemically adapted.

---

### B. Fragment orbitals as coarse variables

For large molecules or molecular assemblies, one can use fragment orbitals:

[
\psi(\mathbf r)
===============

\sum_{A,m}
c_{A m},\varphi_{A m}(\mathbf r)
]

where (A) is a fragment and (m) labels a few important local orbitals: HOMO, LUMO, lone pair, π orbital, radical SOMO, etc.

Then the coarse problem is a tight-binding-like Hamiltonian between fragment states:

[
H^{\rm coarse}_{A m,B n}
========================

\langle \varphi_{A m}|H|\varphi_{B n}\rangle .
]

This is often the most intuitive molecular analogue of coarse elasticity.

Elasticity coarse node: controls smooth displacement of a region.
Electronic fragment orbital: controls low-energy electronic amplitude on a region.

The local high-energy intra-fragment polarization is analogous to the “local disorder” you mentioned in elasticity. It is smoothed out or integrated out.

This gives an effective Hamiltonian:

[
H_{\rm eff}
===========

H_{PP}
+
H_{PQ}
(E-H_{QQ})^{-1}
H_{QP}
]

where (P) is your coarse subspace and (Q) is the eliminated fine subspace. This is Löwdin downfolding / Schur complement. It is the rigorous version of “coarse projection”.

---

### C. Density matrix / occupied subspace as coarse object

For ground-state electronic structure, the object you really want is often not individual eigenvectors but the occupied density matrix:

[
\rho = f(H).
]

In an insulator,

[
\rho(\mathbf r,\mathbf r')
]

decays exponentially with distance. Then locality itself becomes the coarse principle. You can solve local patches and stitch them together. This is related to linear-scaling DFT, Fermi operator expansion, purification, density-matrix minimization, and localized Wannier functions.

For metals, the density matrix decays slowly, so the hierarchy becomes harder. Then you need either temperature smearing, Fermi-surface-adapted basis functions, or explicit coarse treatment of long-range metallic modes.

## 6. Your resolvent problem: a good solver should separate resonant subspace from everything else

You wrote:

[
(EI-H)r=b.
]

This is exactly where a **coarse spectral subspace** is useful.

Let (V) contain approximate eigenvectors near energy (E). Then write

[
r = V y + z
]

with (z) orthogonal to (V). Project:

[
A = EI-H.
]

The coarse equation is

[
(V^\dagger A V)y = V^\dagger b.
]

Then the residual equation is

[
A z =
b - A V y.
]

But now the most dangerous near-null modes have been removed from (z). Jacobi or Krylov on (z) is much better conditioned.

This is called **deflation**. In multigrid language, (V) is the coarse prolongation operator (P).

So the multigrid-like correction is:

[
r \leftarrow r + V
(V^\dagger A V)^{-1}
V^\dagger
(b-Ar).
]

That is the central formula.

For a non-orthogonal basis with overlap (S), the resolvent is usually

[
(ES-H)c=b.
]

Then the projected coarse operator is

[
A_c = V^\dagger (ES-H)V
]

or, if using the (S)-inner product carefully, one uses (V^\dagger S V) normalization. But the idea is the same.

## 7. Where do the coarse electronic vectors (V) come from?

This is the important practical question.

They can come from several places.

For a molecule, (V) can be:

[
V = {\text{fragment HOMOs/LUMOs, lone pairs, π orbitals, radical SOMOs}}.
]

For a periodic solid, (V) can be:

[
V = {\text{Bloch waves near selected } k,E}
]

or localized Wannier functions.

For a real-space grid, (V) can be low-frequency Fourier modes, wavelets, or adaptive local eigenmodes.

For a graph/tight-binding Hamiltonian, (V) can be block-local eigenvectors of small patches. This one is probably most interesting for your GPU thinking.

Take each block (B), solve a small local eigenproblem:

[
H_B u_{B\alpha} = \varepsilon_{B\alpha} u_{B\alpha}
]

and keep only local modes with (\varepsilon_{B\alpha}) close to (E). Then assemble these into a global coarse basis.

This is directly analogous to your local patch projective-dynamics idea. But instead of coarse geometric modes, the patch exports its near-resonant electronic modes.

The prolongation (P) maps coarse coefficients (c_{B\alpha}) to fine atomic coefficients:

[
\psi_i
======

\sum_{B,\alpha}
P_{i,B\alpha} c_{B\alpha}.
]

The coarse Hamiltonian is

[
H_c = P^\dagger H P.
]

The coarse resolvent is

[
(EI_c-H_c)y = P^\dagger b.
]

Then prolongate:

[
r_{\rm coarse}=P y.
]

This is a very clean molecular/electronic multigrid analogue.

## 8. Relation to Lanczos

Lanczos also builds a low-rank subspace, but dynamically from the source (b):

[
\mathcal K_m(H,b)
=================

\mathrm{span}{b,Hb,H^2b,\ldots,H^{m-1}b}.
]

For the resolvent,

[
r=(E-H)^{-1}b,
]

Lanczos is often excellent because it builds the part of the spectrum that (b) actually couples to.

The difference:

**Multigrid/coarse basis:** predefined or geometry/chemistry-based.
**Lanczos/Krylov:** generated adaptively from (b).
**Best practical method:** combine them.

Use a coarse/deflation subspace for known troublesome modes, then run Krylov on the remaining residual.

For example:

[
r \approx V y + Q_m z
]

where (V) is chemical/fragment/patch basis and (Q_m) is Krylov basis.

This is especially useful when (E) lies in the middle of the spectrum. Pure Jacobi is bad. Pure Lanczos may need many iterations if there are many nearby states. Deflated Lanczos/GMRES/MINRES can be much better.

## 9. FMM-like electronic structure?

There is also an analogy to FMM for electronic Hamiltonians, but it is weaker.

The Coulomb part of electronic structure,

[
J[\rho](\mathbf r)
==================

\int
\frac{\rho(\mathbf r')}{|\mathbf r-\mathbf r'|}
,d\mathbf r'
]

is exactly suitable for FMM/multigrid/FFT.

But the kinetic operator and exchange/correlation/orthogonality constraints are not just long-range Coulomb interactions.

For Hartree-Fock exchange,

[
K(\mathbf r,\mathbf r')
\sim
\frac{\rho(\mathbf r,\mathbf r')}{|\mathbf r-\mathbf r'|}
]

there are hierarchical low-rank methods, but they depend strongly on locality of the density matrix.

For tight-binding / DFTB / Hückel-like Hamiltonians, (H) is sparse, so the matrix-vector product is already cheap. The difficulty is not evaluating (H r); the difficulty is inverting (E-H) near the spectrum. That is a spectral problem, not a far-field summation problem.

So for your equation,

[
(EI-H)r=b,
]

FMM is not the main analogy. The better analogy is:

> electronic multigrid = coarse spectral/chemical subspace + local relaxation of residual.

## 10. A useful mental classification

There are three different “coarsenings”:

### Geometric coarsening

Used when DOFs live on real-space nodes and low-frequency errors are spatially smooth.

Examples:

[
\nabla^2 \phi = \rho
]

elasticity,

[
-\nabla\cdot C\nabla u = f
]

real-space Schrödinger at low energy, sometimes.

### Multipole / hierarchical low-rank coarsening

Used when interactions between distant clusters are smooth and low-rank.

Examples:

[
1/r
]

Coulomb, gravity, electrostatic parts of DFT/HF.

### Spectral / algebraic coarsening

Used when troublesome modes are not simply smooth in real space.

Examples:

[
(E-H)r=b
]

electronic Green functions, indefinite Helmholtz-like problems, near-degenerate molecular orbitals, metallic systems.

Electronic structure mostly needs the third type, plus sometimes the first and second.

## 11. Direct analogy to molecular bending/breathing modes

You mentioned bending/breathing modes of molecules. There is a nice parallel:

In molecular mechanics, low-frequency modes are collective nuclear displacements.

In electronic structure, low-energy electronic modes are collective charge/bond-order/orbital rearrangements.

For π systems, coarse electronic variables could be:

[
\delta q_i
]

site charge,

[
\delta p_{ij}
]

bond order / Kekulé distortion,

[
\theta_i
]

local π-orbital phase,

[
c_A
]

fragment orbital amplitude,

[
\Delta_i
]

local Peierls/dimerization order parameter.

For aromatic/conjugated systems, the relevant long-wavelength electronic modes are often not literal long-wavelength charge density, but smooth variations of **phase**, **bond order**, or **sublattice polarization**.

For graphene-like systems near Dirac points, the coarse fields are envelope functions:

[
\psi(\mathbf r)
===============

e^{i\mathbf K\cdot \mathbf r} F_K(\mathbf r)
+
e^{i\mathbf K'\cdot \mathbf r} F_{K'}(\mathbf r).
]

The fast oscillation is fixed by lattice-scale Bloch factors; the slow coarse variables are envelope functions (F_K,F_{K'}). This is exactly an electronic multiscale decomposition.

So in electronic systems the coarse deformation is often:

[
\text{fast atomic orbital pattern}
\times
\text{slow envelope amplitude}.
]

That is the electronic analogue of smooth mesh deformation.

## 12. Concrete algorithm for your resolvent

Suppose you have a sparse tight-binding / DFTB / Hückel Hamiltonian (H), and you want

[
(E+i\eta-H)r=b.
]

Let

[
A = E+i\eta-H.
]

A practical two-level algorithm:

1. Partition atoms into blocks.
2. For each block, diagonalize local Hamiltonian (H_B).
3. Keep (m) local eigenvectors closest to (E).
4. Assemble prolongation matrix (P).
5. Build coarse matrix:

[
A_c = P^\dagger A P.
]

6. Initial coarse solve:

[
A_c y = P^\dagger b.
]

7. Prolongate:

[
r_0 = P y.
]

8. Iterate residual correction:

[
e_c =
A_c^{-1}P^\dagger(b-Ar)
]

[
r \leftarrow r + P e_c
]

plus local smoother or Krylov correction.

In pseudocode form:

```text
A = (E + i*eta) I - H

# Local coarse basis
for block B:
    HB = restrict(H, B)
    eigvals, eigvecs = diagonalize(HB)
    keep eigenvectors with eigvals near E
    insert into P

Ac = P† A P

r = P solve(Ac, P† b)

for iter:
    res = b - A r

    # coarse correction
    yc = solve(Ac, P† res)
    r += P yc

    # local smoother / Krylov step
    r += local_block_smoother(A, res)
```

This is basically **domain-decomposition deflated Green-function solve**.

For GPU, this has a nice structure:

* local block eigensolves are small and independent;
* (P^\dagger H P) is sparse-ish if blocks interact locally;
* the coarse matrix may be much smaller;
* residual smoothing is local;
* only coarse solve/global synchronization is expensive.

This matches your instinct from block-Jacobi and local patch methods.

## 13. But there is one important trap

For elliptic PDEs, coarse grids are universal. Low-frequency error always looks smooth.

For electronic Hamiltonians, the correct coarse space is energy-dependent.

A coarse basis good for valence states may be bad for high-energy conduction states. A basis good near the HOMO may be bad near a σ* resonance. A basis good for π states may miss lone-pair or Rydberg-like states.

So for resolvents, the coarse space should depend on (E):

[
P = P(E).
]

That is why local eigenvectors near (E), fragment orbitals near (E), or Krylov vectors from (b) are better than purely geometric interpolation.

This is also why algebraic multigrid for indefinite Helmholtz/Schrödinger problems is much harder than for Poisson or elasticity.

## 14. A compact physical picture

For elasticity:

[
\text{fine displacement}
========================

\text{coarse deformation}
+
\text{local strain noise}.
]

For QEq/electrostatics:

[
\text{fine charge}
==================

\text{block monopoles/dipoles}
+
\text{local charge rearrangement}.
]

For electronic structure:

[
\text{wavefunction/response}
============================

\text{near-resonant orbital subspace}
+
\text{off-resonant local polarization}.
]

That last line is probably the most useful conceptual bridge.

The long-wavelength modes of electronic structure are not always long in real space. They are “long-lived” or “near-null” in spectral space: states for which

[
E-\varepsilon_n \approx 0.
]

Therefore the electronic multigrid coarse problem should represent the near-resonant spectral components.

# 1. Zeroth method: projected active-space update

Assume first that (L_0) is orthonormal:

[
L_0^\dagger L_0 = I.
]

You have

[
H_0 L_0 = L_0 \varepsilon_0,
]

where

[
\varepsilon_0 =
\operatorname{diag}(\varepsilon_1,\ldots,\varepsilon_m).
]

Then the projected Hamiltonian in the stored frontier space is

[
H_{\rm act}
===========

# L_0^\dagger H L_0

\varepsilon_0
+
L_0^\dagger \Delta H L_0.
]

This is only an (m\times m) matrix. Diagonalize it:

[
H_{\rm act} U = U\varepsilon .
]

Then the updated orbitals are approximated by

[
L = L_0 U.
]

This is the cheapest and cleanest first step.

It captures exactly the **rotation and splitting of the stored frontier orbitals** due to (\Delta H). For example, if your HOMO/HOMO-1/LUMO/LUMO+1 manifold is nearly degenerate and an external field mixes them, this method handles that well.

But it cannot describe polarization of a frontier orbital into any state not present in (L_0).

That missing part is the analogue of the “local disorder” or “fine correction” in multigrid.

# 2. The residual tells you whether the active subspace is enough

[
R = H L - L\varepsilon .

Since (L=L_0U), and since (H_0L_0=L_0\varepsilon_0), the residual is mostly

[
R =
(I-L_0L_0^\dagger)\Delta H L.
]

So the residual directly measures how strongly (\Delta H) pushes the frontier orbitals outside the stored space.

This is the key diagnostic.

If

[
|R_i| \ll \Delta_i
]

where (\Delta_i) is the energy distance from orbital (i) to omitted states, then the active-space approximation is good.

If not, your stored frontier space is too small, or you need residual correction.

This is the electronic analogue of saying:

> the coarse deformation was not enough; fine modes were excited.

---

# 3. First-order correction outside the stored subspace

For each projected orbital (\phi_i), solve approximately

[
(H-\varepsilon_i I)\delta \phi_i = -R_i
]

with the constraint

[
L^\dagger \delta \phi_i = 0.
]

Then update

[
\phi_i \leftarrow \phi_i + \delta\phi_i.
]

This is the standard correction equation behind Davidson / Jacobi-Davidson / Sternheimer perturbation theory.

More explicitly, define the projector

[
P = LL^\dagger,
\qquad
Q = I-P.
]

Then the correction equation is

[
Q(H-\varepsilon_i I)Q,\delta\phi_i = -R_i.
]

This is the rigorous way to let the frontier orbital deform into the omitted basis without storing all eigenvectors of (H_0).

You only need sparse matrix-vector products with (H), which is exactly what you have.

---

# 4. Why this is better than storing all (C_0[N,N])

The perturbative expression would normally be

[
\delta \phi_i
=============

\sum_{a\notin L_0}
\frac{
\phi_a^{(0)}
\langle \phi_a^{(0)}|\Delta H|\phi_i\rangle
}{
\varepsilon_i-\varepsilon_a^{(0)}
}.
]

But this requires all omitted eigenvectors (\phi_a^{(0)}), which you do not want to store.

The correction-equation form avoids that:

[
(H_0-\varepsilon_i I)\delta\phi_i
=================================

-Q\Delta H\phi_i.
]

It gives the same physics but works directly in the sparse AO/site basis.

So instead of explicitly summing over all frozen sigma states, you solve their net response implicitly.

That is very close to a frozen-core / active-space method, but with an optional “core polarization” correction.

---

# 5. Practical hierarchy of methods

I would organize the solver into three levels.

## Level 0: pure active-space Rayleigh-Ritz

Use only

[
H_{\rm act}
===========

\varepsilon_0 + L_0^\dagger \Delta H L_0.
]

Diagonalize (m\times m). Return

[
L=L_0U.
]

This is extremely cheap.

Good when (\Delta H) mainly mixes frontier orbitals among themselves.

Bad when external field, SCC charge response, or geometry deformation strongly couples frontier π states to omitted σ or higher π states.

---

## Level 1: active-space plus residual correction

After Level 0, compute

[
R=HL-L\varepsilon.
]

Then approximately solve

[
Q(H-\varepsilon_iI)Q\delta_i=-R_i.
]

A cheap Davidson-like preconditioner is

[
\delta_i(a)
\approx
-\frac{R_i(a)}
{H_{aa}-\varepsilon_i}.
]

Here (a) labels AO/site basis components.

Then orthogonalize:

[
\delta_i \leftarrow \delta_i - L(L^\dagger\delta_i).
]

Update:

[
\phi_i \leftarrow \phi_i+\delta_i,
]

normalize, and re-diagonalize the small subspace.

This is already much better than pure projection.

---

## Level 2: adaptive Davidson / block Krylov expansion

Instead of immediately adding (\delta_i) to each orbital and forgetting it, collect correction vectors into a larger temporary subspace:

[
V = [L_0,\ Z_1,\ Z_2,\ldots].
]

Then solve the projected problem

[
V^\dagger H V y = \varepsilon y.
]

After diagonalization,

[
L = VY.
]

Compute residuals again, append new correction vectors, and restart when (V) becomes too large.

This is probably the most robust algorithm for your case.

You store only a small adaptive subspace:

[
V \in \mathbb C^{N\times k},
\qquad
k \sim 2m \text{ to } 5m,
]

not the full (N\times N) eigenvector matrix.

---

# 6. Pseudocode for frontier orbital update

```cpp
// Inputs:
//   H0 sparse
//   dH generated on the fly
//   L0[N,m] frontier eigenvectors of H0
//   eps0[m] eigenvalues of H0 frontier states
//
// Goal:
//   updated frontier orbitals L[N,m] of H = H0 + dH

// --- Level 0: active-space projection ---

B = dH * L0                         // N x m
G = L0^dagger * B                   // m x m

Hact = diag(eps0) + G               // m x m

U, eps = diagonalize(Hact)          // small dense eigensolve

L = L0 * U                          // updated active orbitals

// residual
HL = L0 * diag(eps0) * U + B * U    // because H0 L0 = L0 eps0
R  = HL - L * diag(eps)

// residual norm tells whether active space is enough
for i in 0..m-1:
    err[i] = norm(R[:,i])

if err small:
    return L, eps

// --- Level 1/2: residual correction ---

V = L
orthonormalize(V)

for iter in 0..max_iter:

    W = H * V                       // sparse H0*V + on-the-fly dH*V

    T = V^dagger * W                // k x k projected Hamiltonian

    Y, theta = diagonalize(T)

    X = V * Y[:, selected states]   // N x m Ritz vectors

    HX = W * Y[:, selected states]

    R = HX - X * diag(theta)

    if residuals small:
        return X, theta

    Z = precondition(R, theta)      // Davidson / block-Jacobi / local block inverse

    Z = Z - V * (V^dagger * Z)      // remove old subspace
    Z = orthonormalize(Z)

    V = [V, Z]

    if k too large:
        V = restart_with(X, R, maybe previous directions)
```

This is essentially a **warm-started block Davidson solver**.

It maps very well to GPU because the expensive operations are block sparse matrix-vector products:

[
H V,
]

and dense reductions for

[
V^\dagger H V.
]

The small dense diagonalization is negligible for (k\sim 20)–(100).

---

# 7. For your case, do not think of Jacobi as the solver

Plain Jacobi on

[
(H-\varepsilon I)x=0
]

or

[
(E-H)r=b
]

will be terrible near the spectrum.

But Jacobi can still be useful as a **preconditioner/smoother**.

For example, the Davidson correction

[
\delta_i(a)
===========

-\frac{R_i(a)}{H_{aa}-\varepsilon_i}
]

is basically one diagonally preconditioned Jacobi-like correction.

If you can make a better local preconditioner, even better. For example, for each atom/block, solve a small local system:

[
(H_B-\varepsilon_i I)\delta_B = -R_B.
]

That is the direct analogue of your local patch/block solver in elasticity.

So the hierarchy should be:

[
\boxed{
\text{Rayleigh-Ritz active space}
\rightarrow
\text{residual}
\rightarrow
\text{local/block preconditioner}
\rightarrow
\text{restart}
}
]

Not:

[
\text{Jacobi alone}.
]

---

# 8. What happens to frozen sigma states?

Suppose your stored subspace (L_0) contains only frontier π orbitals.

Let

[
P = L_0L_0^\dagger,
\qquad
Q=I-P.
]

The full Hamiltonian can be block-partitioned:

[
H =
\begin{pmatrix}
H_{PP} & H_{PQ} \
H_{QP} & H_{QQ}
\end{pmatrix}.
]

If you keep only (P), you solve

[
H_{PP} c = \varepsilon c.
]

But the exact downfolded active-space Hamiltonian is energy-dependent:

[
H_{\rm eff}(\varepsilon)
========================

H_{PP}
+
H_{PQ}
(\varepsilon-H_{QQ})^{-1}
H_{QP}.
]

The second term is the effect of frozen/omitted states.

If σ orbitals are far away in energy, then

[
(\varepsilon-H_{QQ})^{-1}
]

is small, and freezing them is okay.

But if an external field or distortion mixes π and σ strongly, or if omitted π* states are nearby, this term matters.

The residual correction / Davidson procedure is exactly an iterative way of approximating this Schur-complement correction without explicitly storing the omitted eigenvectors.

---

# 9. External field case

For a simple orthogonal tight-binding model, an electric field may enter as a diagonal onsite perturbation:

[
\Delta H_{ii}
=============

q,\mathbf E\cdot \mathbf r_i.
]

Then

[
\Delta H L_0
]

is trivial: multiply each AO/site row of (L_0) by the local potential.

The active-space matrix is

[
H_{\rm act}
===========

\varepsilon_0
+
L_0^\dagger V_{\rm field} L_0.
]

This captures Stark splitting and mixing inside the frontier manifold.

However, polarizability often comes from mixing occupied and unoccupied states:

[
\alpha
\sim
\sum_{i\in occ}
\sum_{a\in virt}
\frac{
|\langle a|\hat{\mu}|i\rangle|^2
}{
\varepsilon_a-\varepsilon_i
}.
]

Therefore, if you want polarization response, your (L_0) should include not only HOMOs but also enough low virtual states. Otherwise you freeze out the dominant response channel.

A practical rule:

[
m = m_{\rm occ} + m_{\rm virt}
]

with a buffer of virtual π states above the LUMO.

---

# 10. Geometry deformation / Peierls distortion

For bond distortions, (\Delta H) is usually sparse and off-diagonal:

[
t_{ij}
======

t_{ij}^{0}
+
\Delta t_{ij}.
]

For example,

[
\Delta t_{ij}
\approx
\frac{\partial t}{\partial r}*{ij}
\Delta r*{ij}.
]

Then

[
(\Delta H L_0)_i
================

\sum_{j\in neigh(i)}
\Delta H_{ij} L_{0,j}.
]

This is also GPU-friendly.

For Peierls/Kekulé distortions, the coupling is often very strong inside the π frontier subspace. So the pure projected method may already work surprisingly well, provided (L_0) contains the relevant π band-edge states.

But if bending mixes π and σ due to rehybridization, you need residual correction or a larger active basis.

---

# 11. SCC / SCF update

For SCC-DFTB-like logic, (\Delta H) depends on charge redistribution:

[
\Delta H = \Delta H[\Delta q].
]

Then you have an outer nonlinear loop:

[
L \rightarrow \rho \rightarrow \Delta q \rightarrow \Delta H \rightarrow L.
]

With a frozen core, write

[
\rho
====

\rho_{\rm core}^{0}
+
\rho_{\rm active}.
]

The active density is

[
\rho_{\rm active}
=================

L f(\varepsilon)L^\dagger.
]

Then update charges from

[
\Delta \rho_{\rm active}
========================

## \rho_{\rm active}

\rho_{\rm active}^{0}.
]

Then build SCC correction:

[
\Delta H_{\mu\nu}^{\rm SCC}
\sim
\frac{1}{2}S_{\mu\nu}
\sum_C
(\gamma_{AC}+\gamma_{BC})\Delta q_C
]

for AO (\mu) on atom (A), (\nu) on atom (B), in the usual DFTB-like form.

Then mix:

[
\Delta q^{new}
\leftarrow
(1-\alpha)\Delta q^{old}
+
\alpha \Delta q^{computed}.
]

This gives you an active-space SCC method.

But again, frozen σ states means σ polarization is missing unless you include residual correction or an empirical polarizability term.

---

# 12. Non-orthogonal basis version

In DFTB / AO basis, you usually have

[
H C = S C \varepsilon.
]

Then the resolvent is not

[
(EI-H)r=b
]

but

[
(ES-H)r=b.
]

If (S) is fixed and (L_0) is (S)-orthonormal,

[
L_0^\dagger S L_0 = I,
]

then the projected Hamiltonian is

[
H_{\rm act}
===========

L_0^\dagger H L_0.
]

If both (H) and (S) change under deformation,

[
H = H_0+\Delta H,
\qquad
S = S_0+\Delta S,
]

then solve the small generalized problem

[
H_{\rm act}U
============

S_{\rm act}U\varepsilon,
]

where

[
H_{\rm act}=L_0^\dagger H L_0,
]

[
S_{\rm act}=L_0^\dagger S L_0.
]

The residual is

[
R = H L - S L \varepsilon.
]

The correction equation becomes

[
Q(H-\varepsilon_i S)Q\delta_i=-R_i.
]

All orthogonalization should use the (S)-inner product:

[
\langle x,y\rangle_S = x^\dagger S y.
]

This is important. If you ignore (S), the method may produce fake orbital mixing, especially under geometry deformation.

---

# 13. Resolvent problem with known (L_0)

Now consider

[
(E-H)r=b.
]

Let

[
A = E+i\eta-H.
]

Use a small imaginary broadening (\eta), otherwise the problem is singular or nearly singular inside the spectrum.

The stored frontier orbitals give you a deflation/coarse subspace.

Define

[
P=L_0L_0^\dagger.
]

The coarse resolvent is

[
A_c
===

# L_0^\dagger A L_0

## (E+i\eta)I

## \varepsilon_0

L_0^\dagger\Delta H L_0.
]

Then the low-rank/coarse response is

[
r_c
===

L_0 A_c^{-1} L_0^\dagger b.
]

This is the Green-function response restricted to the frontier subspace.

But for the full solution, use it as a deflation correction:

[
r \leftarrow r
+
L_0 A_c^{-1} L_0^\dagger
(b-Ar).
]

This removes the dangerous near-pole components from the residual.

Then solve the remaining residual with Krylov / Jacobi / block-Jacobi.

So the useful preconditioner is

[
M^{-1}
\approx
L_0 A_c^{-1}L_0^\dagger
+
Q D^{-1} Q,
]

where

[
Q=I-P
]

and (D) is a diagonal or block-local approximation to (A).

This is the resolvent analogue of multigrid:

[
\boxed{
\text{frontier orbital subspace = coarse grid}
}
]

[
\boxed{
\text{Jacobi/block-Jacobi = fine smoother}
}
]

---

# 14. Resolvent pseudocode

```cpp
// Solve A r = b
// A = (E + i*eta) I - H

P = L0

Ac = (E + i*eta) * I_m - diag(eps0) - L0^dagger * dH * L0

// Initial coarse solution
bc = L0^dagger * b
yc = solve(Ac, bc)
r  = L0 * yc

for iter in 0..max_iter:

    res = b - A*r

    if norm(res) < tolerance:
        return r

    // Coarse/frontier correction
    resc = L0^dagger * res
    yc   = solve(Ac, resc)
    r   += L0 * yc

    // Fine smoother / preconditioned Krylov step
    res = b - A*r

    z = block_jacobi_or_local_solve(A, res)

    // remove frontier component from z, otherwise it fights the coarse solve
    z -= L0 * (L0^dagger * z)

    r += omega * z
```

This is the simplest multigrid-like version.

For serious robustness, replace the last Jacobi step by GMRES or BiCGStab using the same preconditioner.

For (\eta=0), (A=E-H) is Hermitian but indefinite. Use MINRES if (E) is outside the spectrum or not too singular. For (\eta>0), (A) is complex shifted and not Hermitian in the usual sense; GMRES or BiCGStab is safer.

---

# 15. Relation to Dyson equation

You can also write

[
G(E)
====

# (E-H)^{-1}

(E-H_0-\Delta H)^{-1}.
]

Formally,

[
G
=

G_0
+
G_0\Delta H G.
]

If you had full (G_0), that would be a Dyson solver.

But you only store frontier (L_0). Therefore you have a low-rank approximation

[
G_0(E)
\approx
L_0
(E-\varepsilon_0)^{-1}
L_0^\dagger
+
G_{\rm bg}(E).
]

Here (G_{\rm bg}) is the omitted background. You can approximate it by diagonal/block-local inverse.

That gives the same preconditioner as above:

[
M^{-1}
======

G_{\rm frontier}
+
G_{\rm background}^{approx}.
]

This is a very good interpretation: your (L_0) stores the dangerous poles; the background is smooth and can be treated crudely.

---

# 16. What can go wrong?

The method is excellent if

[
|\Delta H|*{\rm coupling}
\ll
\Delta*{\rm out},
]

where (\Delta_{\rm out}) is the energy distance from your active frontier window to the omitted states.

It fails if an omitted state crosses into the frontier window.

For example:

* a σ state mixes strongly due to bending,
* an external field pulls a Rydberg/edge/localized state into the gap,
* charge reorganization creates a polaronic in-gap state,
* defect states appear,
* molecule-substrate coupling introduces new resonances.

Then no fixed (m)-dimensional (L_0) can be reliable. The residual will reveal this:

[
|HL-L\varepsilon|
]

will remain large.

The cure is simple: adaptively expand (V) using residual vectors.

---

# 17. Best practical recipe for you

For your stated use case, I would implement this order:

1. Store (L_0[N,m]), (\varepsilon_0[m]), sparse (H_0).
2. For each perturbation (\Delta H), compute

[
B=\Delta H L_0.
]

3. Build

[
G=L_0^\dagger B.
]

4. Diagonalize

[
H_{\rm act}=\varepsilon_0+G.
]

5. Form

[
L=L_0U.
]

6. Compute residual

[
R=HL-L\varepsilon.
]

7. If residual is small, stop.
8. If not, enter block-Davidson mode with temporary subspace

[
V=[L,\text{preconditioned residuals}].
]

9. Restart when (V) becomes too large.

This gives you a solver that is:

* low memory,
* GPU-friendly,
* compatible with sparse (H_0),
* compatible with on-the-fly (\Delta H),
* able to reuse old molecular orbitals,
* able to detect when the active subspace is insufficient.

# 18. The conceptual summary

Your (L_0) is the electronic equivalent of a coarse deformation mesh.

But instead of coarse variables being spatial displacements, they are amplitudes of important frontier orbitals.

The decomposition is:

[
\psi
====

L_0 c
+
\delta\psi_{\rm out}.
]

The first term is the active/coarse electronic deformation.

The second term is the fine correction: polarization into frozen σ, deeper π, higher π*, local bond distortions, etc.

The projected Hamiltonian

[
L_0^\dagger H L_0
]

is the coarse-grid solve.

The residual correction

[
Q(H-\varepsilon S)Q\delta=-R
]

is the fine-grid relaxation.

For the resolvent,

[
L_0
\left[
L_0^\dagger(E-H)L_0
\right]^{-1}
L_0^\dagger
]

is the coarse Green-function pole solver, while Jacobi/block-Jacobi/Krylov handles the smooth off-resonant background.

So the natural algorithm is not plain Jacobi or plain Lanczos, but:

[
\boxed{
\text{active Rayleigh-Ritz}
+
\text{residual-driven Davidson/Krylov correction}
+
\text{frontier-subspace deflation for resolvents}.
}
]

Exactly. These are the two places where a naive low-rank frontier treatment can become unphysical:

[
\text{good for a few eigenvalues}
;\not\Rightarrow;
\text{automatically good for density or Green functions.}

For eigenvectors, a sharp active subspace is often acceptable because you only ask: “what are the new HOMO/LUMO-like vectors?” But for density, polarizability, transport, and vibrational spectral functions, the answer is a **spectral object**, and spectral truncation behaves like Fourier truncation: a hard rectangular window can create artificial oscillations.

# 1. Density: never treat the active frontier subspace as “the whole density”

The total density matrix is

[
P = f(H),
]

where (f) is the occupation function. At zero temperature for electrons,

[
f(\varepsilon)=
\theta(E_F-\varepsilon).
]

In an AO/site basis, roughly,

[
\rho(\mathbf r)
===============

\sum_n f_n |\psi_n(\mathbf r)|^2.
]

Each orbital density (|\psi_n|^2) can be very wavy. The smoothness of the total density comes from cancellations among many occupied orbitals. Therefore if you keep only a few frontier orbitals and let them reorganize while freezing everything else too brutally, you can introduce fake charge waves.

The safe decomposition is not

[
P \approx L_{\rm active} f L_{\rm active}^\dagger.
]

Rather it should be

[
\boxed{
P \approx P_0 + \Delta P_{\rm active}
}
]

where (P_0) is the reference density of (H_0), or the neutral-atom/Harris density, and only the **change** is represented in the active space.

That is crucial. The core/sigma/deep-(\pi) density remains as a smooth background. The active solver only computes the density response near the Fermi level / gap.

---

# 2. Why occupied-occupied rotations do not matter for density

Suppose (L_{\rm occ}) is a set of occupied orbitals. The density projector is

[
P_{\rm occ}=L_{\rm occ}L_{\rm occ}^\dagger.
]

If you rotate occupied orbitals among themselves,

[
L_{\rm occ}'=L_{\rm occ}U,
]

then

[
P_{\rm occ}'=
L_{\rm occ}UU^\dagger L_{\rm occ}^\dagger
=========================================

# L_{\rm occ}L_{\rm occ}^\dagger

P_{\rm occ}.
]

So orbital rotation inside a fully occupied subspace is just a gauge change. It does not change density.

Density change comes mainly from **mixing occupied and unoccupied states**:

[
\delta P_{ia}
\sim
\frac{
\langle i|\Delta H|a\rangle
}{
\varepsilon_i-\varepsilon_a
},
]

where (i) is occupied and (a) is unoccupied.

More generally,

[
\boxed{
\delta P_{ij}
=============

\frac{f_i-f_j}
{\varepsilon_i-\varepsilon_j}
\Delta H_{ij}
}
]

for (i\neq j), in the eigenbasis of (H_0).

This formula is very important. If both orbitals are deep occupied, then (f_i-f_j=0), so their mixing does not affect density to first order. If both are empty, same. Only occupation-different pairs contribute.

Therefore the active space for density response should contain a **buffer of occupied and virtual states around the gap**, not only HOMO and LUMO.

---

# 3. Use smooth spectral windows for response, not hard orbital cutoffs

Let (w_i) be a smooth trust/window function around the frontier region:

[
w_i =
w(\varepsilon_i).
]

For example:

[
w(\varepsilon)=1
\quad\text{near the gap,}
]

then smoothly decays to zero outside some wider energy window.

A simple smoothstep-like window:

[
x=\frac{|\varepsilon-E_F|-E_{\rm inner}}
{E_{\rm outer}-E_{\rm inner}},
]

[
w=
\begin{cases}
1, & x<0, \
1-3x^2+2x^3, & 0<x<1, \
0, & x>1.
\end{cases}
]

Then instead of a hard active-space density response, use a tapered response:

[
\delta P_{ij}^{\rm active}
==========================

w_i w_j
\frac{f_i-f_j}
{\varepsilon_i-\varepsilon_j}
\Delta H_{ij}.
]

This does what you want conceptually: states near the gap are fully active, states in the buffer contribute partially, and deep states are frozen smoothly.

This is the direct spectral analogue of avoiding Gibbs ringing in FFT filtering.

But one subtle point: the window (w_i) is not the physical occupation. It is a **numerical trust weight**. The physical occupation is still (f_i). The window only says how much of the perturbative response you allow your truncated solver to represent.

So I would distinguish:

[
f_i = \text{physical occupation},
]

[
w_i = \text{active-space confidence/window}.
]

The density correction should depend on both.

---

# 4. Better than orbital window: pair window

For density response, the natural object is not a single orbital but a pair (i,j), because response comes from transitions.

So use a pair weight

[
W_{ij}=w_i w_j
]

or more physically,

[
W_{ij}
======

w_{\rm trans}(\varepsilon_i,\varepsilon_j).
]

For example, you may want to keep transitions where one state is occupied near HOMO and the other is virtual near LUMO:

[
W_{ia}\approx 1
\quad
\text{for frontier occupied-virtual pairs}.
]

Deep occupied to high virtual transitions can be smoothly suppressed.

This is especially relevant for PAH/graphene because the (\pi)-band is dense. A hard cutoff at orbital (m) is arbitrary. A transition-weighted window is much more stable.

---

# 5. Active density update in practical form

Suppose you store

[
L_0[N,m],
\qquad
\varepsilon_0[m],
\qquad
f_0[m],
\qquad
w[m].
]

You compute the projected perturbation:

[
\Delta H_{\rm act}
==================

L_0^\dagger \Delta H L_0.
]

Then the first-order density correction in this subspace is

[
\Delta P_{\rm act}
==================

L_0 \Delta F L_0^\dagger,
]

where

[
\Delta F_{ij}
=============

w_i w_j
\frac{f_i-f_j}
{\varepsilon_i-\varepsilon_j}
(\Delta H_{\rm act})_{ij}.
]

For nearly degenerate (i,j), use the derivative form:

[
\frac{f_i-f_j}{\varepsilon_i-\varepsilon_j}
\rightarrow
f'(\varepsilon_i).
]

If you instead diagonalize the active Hamiltonian exactly,

[
H_{\rm act}U=U\varepsilon,
]

then

[
P_{\rm act}^{new}
=================

L_0 U f(\varepsilon) U^\dagger L_0^\dagger,
]

and the density correction is

[
\Delta P_{\rm active}
=====================

L_0
\left[
U f(\varepsilon)U^\dagger
-------------------------

f(\varepsilon_0)
\right]
L_0^\dagger.
]

With a taper, use something like

[
\Delta P_{\rm active}
=====================

L_0
\left[
W \circ
\left(
U f(\varepsilon)U^\dagger
-------------------------

f(\varepsilon_0)
\right)
\right]
L_0^\dagger,
]

where (\circ) is elementwise multiplication, and (W_{ij}=w_iw_j).

This is a reasonable practical active-density formula.

---

# 6. But for SCC/SCF, you may also need background polarizability

If your active space contains only (\pi)-frontier states, it misses the smooth polarization of (\sigma) bonds and deep electrons. For electrostatics/SCC, this missing response can matter.

So I would write the charge response as

[
\Delta q
========

\Delta q_{\pi,\rm active}
+
\Delta q_{\rm bg}.
]

The active part comes from your low-rank frontier solver.

The background can be modeled cheaply, for example by atomic polarizabilities, bond polarizabilities, or QEq-like local response:

[
\Delta q_{\rm bg}
\approx
-\chi_{\rm bg} V_{\rm eff}.
]

This avoids forcing the frontier orbitals to fake all polarization. Otherwise your few HOMO/LUMO states may overpolarize and produce unphysical density waves.

For PAHs, I would expect a good hierarchy to be:

[
P =
P_0
+
\Delta P_{\pi,\rm frontier}
+
\Delta P_{\sigma/\rm deep}^{\rm local\ polarizability}.
]

That is much more physical than “only optimize HOMO/LUMO and ignore everything else.”

---

# 7. Green functions: project only what you need, not (G[N,N,E])

For transport or spectral functions, the full retarded Green function is

[
G^r(E)
======

\left[
(E+i\eta)S
----------

## H

## \Sigma_L(E)

\Sigma_R(E)
\right]^{-1}.
]

You are right: storing

[
G[N,N,n_E]
]

is hopeless and unnecessary.

In Landauer transport, you usually only need

[
T(E)
====

\operatorname{Tr}
\left[
\Gamma_L(E)
G^r(E)
\Gamma_R(E)
G^a(E)
\right],
]

where

[
\Gamma_\alpha(E)
================

i\left[
\Sigma_\alpha(E)-\Sigma_\alpha^\dagger(E)
\right].
]

If (\Sigma_L,\Sigma_R) act only on a few linker/contact atoms, then (\Gamma_L,\Gamma_R) are nonzero only in a small contact subspace.

Therefore you do not need the whole (G). You only need the block

[
G_{C_L,C_R}(E)
]

between left and right contact DOFs.

Equivalently, solve

[
A(E)X = B,
]

where

[
A(E)=
(E+i\eta)S-H-\Sigma_L(E)-\Sigma_R(E),
]

and (B) contains only contact basis vectors.

Then

[
X=A^{-1}B
]

contains the Green-function columns that are actually needed.

So memory is

[
N \times n_{\rm contact},
]

not

[
N\times N.
]

That is the key reduction.

---

# 8. Combine frontier subspace and contact subspace

There is one important trap. If you project only onto frontier orbitals (L_0), you may miss contact physics if the linker atoms couple strongly to orbitals outside (L_0).

Therefore for transport I would not use only

[
V=L_0.
]

I would use

[
\boxed{
V = [,L_0,\ C_L,\ C_R,\ K_1,\ldots,]
}
]

where (C_L,C_R) are localized basis vectors on linker/contact atoms, and (K_1,\ldots) are optional Krylov vectors generated from the contacts:

[
K_1 = H C,
]

[
K_2 = H^2 C,
]

etc., orthogonalized against previous vectors.

This is very natural physically:

* (L_0) captures molecular resonances near the gap,
* (C_L,C_R) capture injection/extraction at electrodes,
* Krylov contact vectors capture short paths from electrodes into the molecule,
* residual iterative solve captures the remaining background.

So the projected Green-function problem becomes

[
G_V(E)
======

\left[
V^\dagger A(E)V
\right]^{-1}.
]

The approximate full-space Green action is

[
G(E)B
\approx
V G_V(E) V^\dagger B.
]

But for transmission you can often stay entirely inside the projected space.

Projected self-energies:

[
\Sigma_V(E)=V^\dagger \Sigma(E)V.
]

Projected broadenings:

[
\Gamma_V(E)=V^\dagger \Gamma(E)V.
]

Then

[
T(E)
\approx
\operatorname{Tr}
\left[
\Gamma_{L,V}
G_V
\Gamma_{R,V}
G_V^\dagger
\right].
]

Now the expensive inverse is only (k\times k), where

[
k = m+n_{\rm contact}+n_{\rm Krylov}.
]

---

# 9. Self-energy on linker atoms is naturally low-rank

Let (C\in\mathbb C^{N\times n_c}) select the contact/linker atomic orbitals.

Then the full self-energy is

[
\Sigma(E)=C,\sigma(E),C^\dagger,
]

where

[
\sigma(E)\in\mathbb C^{n_c\times n_c}.
]

This is exactly low-rank.

If it is diagonal per linker atom, even simpler:

[
\sigma_{ab}(E)=\delta_{ab}\sigma_a(E).
]

Then applying it to a vector costs only

[
O(n_c)
]

plus scatter/gather to full (N)-space.

The projected self-energy is

[
\Sigma_V(E)
===========

V^\dagger C,\sigma(E),C^\dagger V.
]

Define

[
M_C = C^\dagger V,
]

which is just the value of each projected vector on contact atoms. Then

[
\boxed{
\Sigma_V(E)
===========

M_C^\dagger \sigma(E) M_C.
}
]

This is very cheap.

You can precompute (M_C). For each energy (E), you only update the small (n_c\times n_c) (\sigma(E)).

---

# 10. Do not write the Hamiltonian as (H+SE+\Sigma)

For the generalized nonorthogonal electronic problem, the operator to invert is

[
\boxed{
A(E)
====

## (E+i\eta)S

## H

\Sigma(E).
}
]

So the Green function is

[
G(E)=A(E)^{-1}.
]

If you write “Hamiltonian contains (S E),” that can become confusing. Better to keep (H), (S), and (\Sigma(E)) separate:

[
H = H_0 + V_{\rm ext} + V_{\rm SCC} + \Delta H_{\rm geom},
]

[
A(E)
====

## (E+i\eta)S

## H

## \Sigma_L(E)

\Sigma_R(E).
]

If geometry changes overlap too, then

[
S=S_0+\Delta S.
]

Then

[
A(E)
====

## (E+i\eta)(S_0+\Delta S)

## (H_0+\Delta H)

\Sigma(E).
]

This is the clean form.

---

# 11. Woodbury viewpoint: exact low-rank self-energy correction

Suppose you have a background inverse approximately available:

[
G_0(E)=
[(E+i\eta)S-H]^{-1}.
]

And self-energy is low-rank:

[
\Sigma=C\sigma C^\dagger.
]

Then

[
G =
(A_0-C\sigma C^\dagger)^{-1}.
]

Woodbury gives

[
G =
G_0
+
G_0 C
\left[
\sigma^{-1}
-----------

C^\dagger G_0 C
\right]^{-1}
C^\dagger G_0.
]

This says the electrode effect is controlled by the small contact Green matrix

[
g_C(E)=C^\dagger G_0(E) C.
]

Again: no full (N\times N) Green matrix is needed.

In practice, you may not have exact (G_0), but your low-rank (L_0) approximation gives

[
G_0(E)
\approx
L_0
\left[
(E+i\eta)I-\varepsilon_0
\right]^{-1}
L_0^\dagger
+
G_{\rm bg}^{approx}.
]

Then the contact Green matrix is approximately

[
g_C(E)
\approx
(C^\dagger L_0)
\left[
(E+i\eta)I-\varepsilon_0
\right]^{-1}
(L_0^\dagger C)
+
g_{C,\rm bg}^{approx}.
]

This is probably the most compact transport formulation for your case.

# 12. Practical low-rank Landauer algorithm

For each geometry / SCC state:

1. Build/update sparse

[
H=H_0+\Delta H.
]

2. Choose projected basis

[
V=[L_0,\ C_L,\ C_R,\ \text{optional residual/Krylov vectors}].
]

3. Orthonormalize (V), using (S)-inner product if nonorthogonal:

[
V^\dagger S V=I.
]

4. Project:

[
H_V=V^\dagger H V,
]

[
S_V=V^\dagger S V.
]

If (S)-orthonormalized, (S_V=I).

5. For each energy (E), build small matrix

[
A_V(E)=
(E+i\eta)S_V
------------

## H_V

## \Sigma_{L,V}(E)

\Sigma_{R,V}(E).
]

6. Invert small matrix:

[
G_V(E)=A_V(E)^{-1}.
]

7. Compute

[
T(E)=
\operatorname{Tr}
[
\Gamma_{L,V}
G_V
\Gamma_{R,V}
G_V^\dagger
].
]

This avoids full (G[N,N,n_E]). You store only:

[
V[N,k],
]

[
H_V[k,k],
]

[
M_C=C^\dagger V.
]

# 13. But projected transport can miss antiresonances

A warning: transport is more sensitive than eigenvalues.

Transmission depends not only on resonance energies but also on interference paths and contact amplitudes. A low-rank frontier basis can reproduce HOMO/LUMO peaks but miss:

* destructive interference antiresonances,
* σ-mediated tunneling background,
* linker-localized states,
* electrode-induced broadening of non-frontier orbitals,
* evanescent off-resonant paths.

So for transport, I would always augment (L_0) by contact Krylov vectors.

For example:

[
V_0=[L_0,C_L,C_R].
]

Then generate

[
V_1=H V_0,
]

[
V_2=H V_1,
]

orthogonalizing each time.

Even 2–4 Krylov layers from contacts can capture short-range tunneling paths much better than frontier orbitals alone.

This is like a molecular version of recursive Green-function / block Lanczos, but with frontier orbitals injected into the basis from the start.

# 14. Vibrational Green functions: same structure, different operator

For vibrations, the eigenproblem is

[
K u = \omega^2 M u.
]

In mass-weighted coordinates,

[
D q = \omega^2 q,
]

where

[
D = M^{-1/2} K M^{-1/2}.
]

The vibrational Green function is

[
G^r(\omega)
===========

\left[
(\omega+i\eta)^2 I
------------------

## D

## \Pi_L(\omega)

\Pi_R(\omega)
\right]^{-1}.
]

Here (\Pi(\omega)) is the phonon self-energy of the substrate/electrode/bath.

If the molecule is covalently bonded to a surface through a few atoms, then (\Pi) acts only on interface displacement DOFs.

For atoms, each interface atom has 3 displacement DOFs, so

[
n_c = 3 n_{\rm interface atoms}.
]

Again,

[
\Pi(\omega)
===========

C,\pi(\omega),C^\dagger.
]

The phonon transmission is

[
T_{\rm ph}(\omega)
==================

\operatorname{Tr}
[
\Lambda_L
G^r
\Lambda_R
G^a
],
]

with

[
\Lambda_\alpha(\omega)
======================

i[
\Pi_\alpha-\Pi_\alpha^\dagger
].
]

This is exactly analogous to electronic Landauer, with

[
E S-H-\Sigma
]

replaced by

[
\omega^2 I-D-\Pi.
]

---

# 15. Low-rank basis for vibrations

For molecular vibrations, the analogue of frontier orbitals is a set of important normal modes:

[
Q_0[N_{\rm dof},m].
]

For example:

* modes in the frequency range of interest,
* IR-active modes,
* modes localized near the linker,
* soft bending/torsional modes,
* modes coupled to electron transport.

Then use

[
V=[Q_0,C_{\rm interface},D C_{\rm interface},D^2 C_{\rm interface},\ldots].
]

Project:

[
D_V=V^\dagger D V,
]

[
\Pi_V(\omega)=V^\dagger \Pi(\omega)V.
]

Then

[
G_V(\omega)
===========

[
(\omega+i\eta)^2 I
------------------

## D_V

\Pi_V(\omega)
]^{-1}.
]

Again, no full Green matrix.

This is especially good for a molecule bonded to Si/diamond through a few atoms. The substrate only enters through the small interface self-energy.

# 16. Relation between active subspace and self-energy

The self-energy makes the effective Hamiltonian non-Hermitian and energy-dependent:

[
H_{\rm eff}(E)
==============

H+\Sigma(E).
]

So the resonances are not just eigenvalues of (H). They satisfy something like

[
\det[
E S-H-\Sigma(E)
]=0.
]

The imaginary part of (\Sigma) gives broadening:

[
\Gamma(E)=i(\Sigma-\Sigma^\dagger).
]

The real part shifts orbital energies.

If you project onto (L_0), then each frontier orbital gets an energy-dependent correction:

[
\Sigma_{ij}^{\rm act}(E)
========================

\langle \psi_i|\Sigma(E)|\psi_j\rangle.
]

If (\Sigma) acts on linker atoms only,

[
\Sigma_{ij}^{\rm act}
=====================

\sum_{a,b\in C}
\psi_i^*(a)
\sigma_{ab}(E)
\psi_j(b).
]

So the contact coupling of a molecular orbital is controlled by its amplitude on the linker atoms. That gives the usual intuitive result:

* orbital has amplitude on both contacts → conductance peak,
* orbital has node on one contact → dark state,
* two paths interfere → antiresonance.

This is another reason to include contact basis vectors in (V), not only molecular eigenstates. The linker region can mediate interference through orbitals outside the frontier window.

# 17. Suggested unified operator form

For electrons, I would define the energy-dependent operator as

[
\boxed{
A_e(E)
======

## (E+i\eta)S

\left[
H_0
+
V_{\rm ext}
+
V_{\rm SCC}
+
\Delta H_{\rm geom}
\right]
-------

\Sigma_e(E).
}
]

For vibrations:

[
\boxed{
A_{\rm vib}(\omega)
===================

## (\omega+i\eta)^2 M

## K

\Pi(\omega).
}
]

Or in mass-weighted form:

[
A_{\rm vib}(\omega)
===================

## (\omega+i\eta)^2 I

## D

\Pi'(\omega).
]

Then everything is:

[
A(z)x=b.
]

Your low-rank method is:

[
x \approx V y,
]

[
V^\dagger A(z)V,y=V^\dagger b.
]

With optional residual correction:

[
r=b-Ax,
]

[
x\leftarrow x + \text{preconditioned correction}.
]

This is the common abstraction for:

* electronic frontier orbitals,
* SCC response,
* Landauer transport,
* vibrational spectra,
* molecule-surface damping,
* phonon transport.

# 18. The method I would actually implement

I would use a **three-part basis**:

[
\boxed{
V = [,L_{\rm win},\ C,\ K_C,].
}
]

Where:

1. (L_{\rm win}) = spectral-windowed molecular orbitals around the gap.
2. (C) = contact/interface/linker atomic basis functions.
3. (K_C) = short Krylov chain from contacts:

[
C,\ HC,\ H^2C,\ldots
]

or for vibrations:

[
C,\ DC,\ D^2C,\ldots
]

Then all Green/spectral quantities are computed in this compact projected space.

For density/SCC, use:

[
P=P_0+\Delta P_{\rm win}+\Delta P_{\rm bg}.
]

Where:

* (\Delta P_{\rm win}) comes from active orbitals with smooth spectral taper,
* (\Delta P_{\rm bg}) is local polarizability / QEq / empirical background response.

This avoids both major artifacts:

1. artificial density ringing from sharp spectral cutoff,
2. missing transport/interface physics from frontier-only projection.

# 19. The core intuition

Your low-rank frontier basis (L_0) is excellent for the **poles** of the Green function:

[
G(E)
\sim
\sum_n
\frac{
|\psi_n\rangle\langle\psi_n|
}{
E-\varepsilon_n+i\eta
}.
]

Near the HOMO/LUMO, only a few poles dominate. That is why (L_0) works.

But density and transport also depend on:

[
\text{background continuum}
+
\text{contact injection}
+
\text{off-resonant tunneling}
+
\text{polarization of omitted states}.
]

Therefore the best compact representation is not just

[
L_0.
]

It is

[
\boxed{
\text{frontier poles}
+
\text{smooth background response}
+
\text{contact/interface subspace}.
}
]

That is the multigrid/effective-medium picture in electronic language.
